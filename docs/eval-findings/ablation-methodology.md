# Ablation Methodology Defense: Why ParBench Uses pass@1

**Date:** 2026-04-26
**Purpose:** Literature-grounded defense of the ParBench ablation experiment design for NeurIPS 2026 reviewer challenges.
**Authoritative experiment spec:** `docs/neurips2026-experiment-plan.md` Section 2.4

---

## 1. Executive Summary

ParBench ablation studies use **pass@1, num_samples=1, augmentation levels L1-L4**, restricted to cells that pass at L0 (no augmentation). This design is standard practice across the entire code generation evaluation literature. A survey of 10 papers spanning 2020-2024 across 5 venues (NeurIPS, ICLR, ACL, HPDC, arXiv) found that **every single one uses pass@1 (or a single-attempt equivalent) for ablation and robustness studies**. No paper in the surveyed corpus uses pass@3 or any multi-sample best-of-k metric for ablation.

Current experiment status:
- Qwen 3.5 397B ablation: complete (51 L0-passer cells x 4 levels = 204 results)
- GPT-5.4 ablation: 99 L0-passer cells x 4 levels = 396 results (launching)

Papers that report pass@k with k>1 (Chen et al., CodeRosetta) do so for **headline capability reporting**, not for ablation comparisons. ## 3. Why pass@1 for Ablation (Methodological Argument)

### 3.1 Ablations measure component contribution, not capability ceiling

pass@k with k>1 answers "can the model ever succeed in k tries?" This is a **capability ceiling** question appropriate for headline reporting. Ablations ask a different question: "does this component (source code augmentation) degrade performance?" This is a **component contribution** question best answered by typical-case pass@1.

Using pass@3 for ablation conflates two effects: the augmentation's difficulty effect and the sampling diversity benefit of taking the best of 3 independent tries. If a model's pass@3 stays high under augmentation but pass@1 drops, the augmentation genuinely hurt the model — the high pass@3 merely reflects that one lucky sample out of three can still succeed. This masks the signal the ablation is designed to measure.

### 3.2 The Chen et al. 2021 temperature finding

Chen et al. (2107.03374, Section 4.2) demonstrated that pass@1-optimal temperature (~0.2) diverges significantly from pass@100-optimal temperature (~0.8). ParBench canonical runs use temp=0.7 (optimized for pass@3 sampling diversity). Using pass@3 at temp=0.7 for ablation would conflate two confounded effects: the augmentation's impact on code quality and the temperature's interaction with best-of-k selection. pass@1 isolates the augmentation effect.

### 3.3 Apples-to-apples paired comparison

The ParBench ablation design achieves a clean paired comparison:

- **Baseline:** pass@1 at L0, derived from the first canonical sample (s0, already collected)
- **Treatment:** pass@1 at L1, L2, L3, L4 (one fresh sample each)
- **Metric:** delta_pass@1(L_k) = pass@1_L0 - pass@1_L_k

Both terms use the same metric on the same cell set. The canonical pass@3 is used exclusively for headline reporting ("how good is this model?") and does not enter the ablation analysis.

## 4. Statistical Power Analysis

### GPT-5.4 ablation design

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| L0-passer cells | 99 |
| Samples per cell per level | 1 |
| Augmentation levels | 4 (L1, L2, L3, L4) |
| Total API calls | 396 |
| Baseline pass rate (L0) | 1.0 (by construction — L0-conditional filter) |

### Standard error estimates

At a hypothetical degraded pass rate p = 0.35 (typical L3-L4 range based on ReCode findings):

- SE = sqrt(p(1-p)/N) = sqrt(0.35 x 0.65 / 99) = 0.048
- 95% CI: p +/- 1.96 x SE = +/- 9.4 percentage points

### Detection power

The expected L0-to-L4 degradation is 15-30 percentage points (based on ReCode's 20-40% robustness drop on perturbation benchmarks). With SE = 4.8pp, ParBench can detect a 10pp drop at 95% confidence — well within the expected effect size.

### Qwen comparison

| Parameter | GPT-5.4 | Qwen 3.5 397B |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| L0-passer cells | 99 | 51 |
| SE at p=0.35 | 0.048 | 0.067 |
| 95% CI width | +/- 9.4pp | +/- 13.1pp |

GPT-5.4's larger L0-passer set (99 vs 51) provides ~30% tighter confidence intervals than Qwen. Both are sufficient for detecting the expected degradation magnitude.

### Limitations (acknowledge in threats-to-validity)

- n=1 sample per cell may not resolve fine-grained L1 vs L2 differences (expected delta < 5pp)
- The 95% CI of +/- 9.4pp is wider than typical HumanEval studies (n=200, SE ~3pp), but ParBench's 87-kernel x 6-direction design provides comparable statistical mass through the diversity of translation tasks
- The L0-conditional filter means ablation results describe "robustness among already-successful translations," not population-level performance

## 5. ParBench Experiment Design Summary

### Canonical (Phase A)
- **Metric:** pass@3 (3 independent samples, temp=0.7)
- **Augmentation:** L0 (none — original source code)
- **Scope:** 87 non-KNOWN_FAIL kernels x 6 translation directions = 522 cells
- **Models:** Qwen 3.5 397B (504 results, 36.7% pass) + GPT-5.4 (426 results, 62.7% pass)
- **Purpose:** Headline capability reporting

### Ablation (Phase C)
- **Metric:** pass@1 (1 sample, temp=0.7)
- **Augmentation:** L1, L2, L3, L4 (4 levels of increasing source code perturbation)
- **Scope:** L0-passer cells only (cells where at least 1 of 3 canonical samples passed)
- **Models:** Qwen (51 cells x 4 = 204 results, complete) + GPT-5.4 (99 cells x 4 = 396 results, launching)
- **Purpose:** Robustness measurement — "how fragile is translation quality under source perturbation?"

### Robustness metric

delta_pass@1(L_k) = pass@1_canonical(L0) - pass@1(L_k)

Both terms restricted to the L0-passer cell set. pass@1 at L0 is derived from the first canonical sample (s0), giving a consistent single-sample baseline for comparison.

### Design rationale for L0-conditional filter

Cells that fail at L0 (unaugmented source) provide no information about robustness under perturbation — they would fail regardless of augmentation level. Including them would:
1. Bias the ablation denominator (inflating apparent robustness by including always-failing cells)
2. Waste budget on uninformative API calls (~$500 additional cost for the full grid)

This filter is both scientifically motivated and budget-efficient.

## 6. Reviewer Defense Points

| Anticipated Objection | Response |
|----------------------|----------|
| "Why not pass@3 for ablation?" | No paper in the code generation evaluation literature uses pass@3 for ablation studies. All 10 surveyed papers (spanning 2020-2024, 5 venues) use pass@1 or equivalent single-attempt metrics. pass@3 would conflate the augmentation effect with sampling diversity, masking the signal the ablation measures. |
| "Is n=1 sample per cell sufficient?" | The paired comparison design (same kernel, canonical vs augmented) partially compensates for per-cell variance. The 4-level degradation curve shape (L1 through L4) is more informative than any single point estimate. The overall SE of +/- 9.4pp (GPT-5.4) and +/- 13.1pp (Qwen) is sufficient to detect the expected 15-30pp degradation. We acknowledge the limitation in threats-to-validity. |
| "Why the L0-conditional filter?" | Cells that fail at L0 (no augmentation) tell us nothing about robustness under perturbation — they would fail regardless. Including them would bias the ablation denominator and inflate apparent robustness. This is both scientifically motivated (measuring degradation requires a working baseline) and budget-efficient ($124 vs ~$650 for full grid). |
| "How do you compare pass@3 canonical to pass@1 ablation?" | We do not compare them directly. pass@1 at L0 is derived from the first canonical sample (already collected), giving an apples-to-apples pass@1-vs-pass@1 comparison. The canonical pass@3 is for headline reporting only. See Section 2.3 of `docs/neurips2026-experiment-plan.md`. |
| "Why temp=0.7 for ablation if Chen et al. say pass@1 optimal is ~0.2?" | We use temp=0.7 consistently across canonical and ablation to isolate the augmentation variable. Lowering temperature for ablation would introduce a confound: any performance change could reflect the temperature shift rather than the augmentation. The paired design controls for this. |
| "Can you detect small effects between adjacent augmentation levels?" | Honestly, no — and we say so. With SE ~5pp, the L1-vs-L2 difference (expected <5pp) is below our detection threshold. The ablation is designed to detect the overall L0-to-L4 degradation trend and identify which models are more robust, not to rank individual augmentation levels. The degradation curve shape (monotonic? cliff? plateau?) is the primary analysis target. |
