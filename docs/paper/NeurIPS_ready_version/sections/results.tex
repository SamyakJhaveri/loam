\section{Results}

\subsection{Overall Performance}
\label{sec:overall-pass}

% src: quantitative_findings_qwen.json > canonical > aggregate_pass_rates > overall: value=0.3674, ci=[0.3306, 0.4059], n=626
% src: statistical_analysis.json > model_comparison > pairwise[0] (Bonferroni-corrected p)
Kernel-centric evaluation isolates executable kernel translation from repository reconstruction. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:overall-pass}, \qwenshort{} passes 36.7\% of its valid records, while \gptnew{} passes 75.5\%. \codex{} passes 74.2\% of its valid records, making it statistically indistinguishable from \gptnew{} (Fisher's exact $p = 1.0$, OR\,=\,0.93 [0.74, 1.16], Cohen's $h = -0.031$). Code-specialized reinforcement learning training does not measurably improve parallel translation success at this task difficulty.
Prior work such as ParEval-Repo reports 0\% pass@1 on repository-level applications larger than 133 SLoC \cite{ParEvalRepo2025}. While 87\% of our evaluated kernels exceed that length, \parbench{}'s kernel isolation reveals baseline translation capability independent of full-repository integration errors. For example, \qwenshort{} achieves 40.3\% on CUDA-to-OpenMP at L0.

\subsection{pass@$k$ Analysis}
\label{sec:passk-analysis}
% src: paper_data_azure-gpt-5.3-codex.json > passk_campaign > aggregate_passk
Restricting to the 142 L0 tasks to estimate single-sample vs. multi-sample success, \qwenshort{} achieves 23.9\% pass@1 and 35.2\% pass@3, while \codex{} achieves pass@1\,=\,62.7\% and pass@3\,=\,68.3\%, matching \gptnew{}'s pass@1 exactly with a slightly narrower pass@1-to-pass@3 gap (5.6\,pp versus 7.0\,pp).(Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-e4}). Additional stochastic samples provide diminishing returns. Of the 142 tasks, 64.8\% produce no passing sample across three independent attempts, and 13.4\% pass in all three. Failures are largely systematic (incorrect API mapping) rather than stochastic; resampling is insufficient to recover most failing pairs.

Per-record pass rates vary strongly across directions (Table~\ref{tab:direction-rates}), reflecting structural API asymmetries. Translating from CUDA to OpenMP replaces explicit device memory and launch configuration with compiler directives. The reverse requires introducing explicit synchronization and memory management. OpenCL translations introduce further complexity with separate host/kernel source and JIT compilation, leading to 0\% success for OpenCL-to-CUDA under \qwenshort{}. \codex{}'s direction profile nearly mirrors \gptnew{} across all six standard directions (within 2--10\,pp), reinforcing that direction difficulty is a property of the task, not the model. At the kernel level, performance is highly heterogeneous (full breakdown in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-e4}). Simple kernels pass at high rates, while those with irregular memory access or multi-file dependencies frequently fail entirely.

Build-stage adaptation is the dominant failure mode, accounting for 39.1\% of all records and 61.9\% of failures for \qwenshort{} (Figure~\ref{fig:failure-taxonomy}). These indicate incomplete API-surface adaptation (e.g., retained CUDA identifiers in OpenMP targets). \verifyfail{} accounts for 4.6\% of records, proving that compile-only or zero-exit-code checks are insufficient for rigorous evaluation. \runfail{} represents 19.3\% of records, frequently driven by OpenCL JIT compilation errors occurring at runtime. \codex{}'s failure distribution is intermediate: \buildfail{} accounts for 17.1\% of records, between \qwenshort{}'s 39.1\% and \gptnew{}'s 15.0\%.

\subsection{Augmentation Robustness}
\label{sec:augmentation-robustness}
% src: statistical_analysis.json > augmentation_curves > azure-gpt-5.3-codex; azure-gpt-5.4; together-qwen-3.5-397b-a17b

To assess whether baseline success is driven primarily by surface-form memorization, we evaluated augmented source variants (L1--L4) on L0-conditional subsets (detailed in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-e4}). \codex{} shows the same plateau pattern as \gptnew{}: 86.6\%--88.7\% at L1--L4 ($\chi^2$ independence test, $p = 1.0$ after Bonferroni correction), in contrast to \qwenshort{}'s peak-then-decline (74.0\% at L1 to 56.0\% at L4, $p = 0.005$). This stability across both GPT models is compatible with robustness to surface-form perturbation, though survivorship bias (L0-conditional filtering) prevents ruling out deeper structural memorization entirely.

% src: results/evaluation/together-qwen-3.5-397b-a17b/hecbench-stencil1d-cuda-to-hecbench-stencil1d-omp-{s0,L2,L4}.json 
Per-kernel analysis reveals exceptions: on \texttt{stencil1d}, \qwenshort{} produces a correct abstracted parallel loop at L0 but reverts to a non-portable CUDA-style tiling pattern under augmentation (L2, L4), yielding verification failures--an instance where surface-form perturbation exposes fragile pattern-matching rather than semantic reasoning.
