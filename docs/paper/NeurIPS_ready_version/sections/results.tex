Kernel-centric evaluation isolates executable kernel translation from repository reconstruction. \qwenshort{} passes 230 of 626 valid records, or 36.7\%~[33.1\%, 40.6\%], across five benchmark suites, ten translation directions, and five augmentation levels (Table~\ref{tab:overall-pass}).
% src: quantitative_findings_qwen.json > canonical > aggregate_pass_rates > per_suite
Per-suite rates range from 65.2\% (HeCBench, $n{=}184$) through 28.2\% (Rodinia, $n{=}380$) and 9.1\% (mixbench, $n{=}22$) to 4.6\% (XSBench, $n{=}22$) and 0\% (RSBench, $n{=}18$), reflecting the heterogeneous difficulty of the curated corpus.

The comparison to repository-level work is the main methodological point. ParEval-Repo reports 0\% pass@1 for all tested models on repository-level applications larger than 133~SLoC~\cite{ParEvalRepo2025}.
% src: sloc_analysis.json: 27 of 31 evaluated kernels above ParEval-Repo 133 SLoC threshold
Of the 31 evaluated kernels, 27 (87\%) exceed that threshold (Section~\ref{sec:benchmark-curation}), but \parbench{} fixes host code, build infrastructure, and I/O---isolating kernel translation from repository reconstruction. Under this isolation, \qwenshort{} achieves 36.7\% overall and 40.3\%~[29.7\%, 51.8\%] on CUDA-to-OpenMP at L0.

This does not make the task easy; it separates kernel translation failures from full-repository reconstruction failures.

The 36.7\% per-record rate in Table~\ref{tab:overall-pass} treats each sample and augmentation level independently. The pass@$k$ analysis below restricts to the 142 L0 tasks and macro-averages to estimate single-sample and three-sample success probabilities. Each of the 142 tasks generates three independent samples at temperature~0.7, yielding 426 L0 records that contribute to the per-record rate.

Additional stochastic samples partially recover only 31 of 142 tasks and none of the 92 hard failures. Of 142 unique L0 source-target pairs, 92 (64.8\%) produce no passing sample across three independent attempts (Table~\ref{tab:pass-at-k-main}). Only 19 tasks (13.4\%) pass in all three samples, while the remaining 31 (21.8\%) are noisy---passing in some samples but not others. The gap between pass@1 (23.9\%) and pass@3 (35.2\%) is modest, indicating that additional samples yield diminishing returns. Limited stochastic resampling is insufficient to recover most task-direction pairs.

% src: quantitative_findings_qwen.json > canonical > direction_pass_rates > standard
Per-record pass rates vary across directions (Table~\ref{tab:direction-rates}). Among the six standard directions, CUDA-to-OpenMP passes at 40.3\% while OpenCL-to-CUDA yields 0\% across all 57 L0 records. This pattern is consistent with the structural asymmetry of the APIs: translating from CUDA to OpenMP replaces explicit device memory allocation, host-device data transfers, and kernel launch configuration with directive-based parallelism where the compiler manages thread scheduling. Conversely, translating into CUDA or OpenCL requires coherently introducing those explicit constructs. OpenCL translations additionally face a separate host/kernel source model and address-space qualifiers (\texttt{\_\_global}, \texttt{\_\_local}) in kernel source that is JIT-compiled via \texttt{clBuildProgram()}. Of the 121 \runfail{} records, 56 involve OpenCL JIT compilation errors at \texttt{clBuildProgram()}, which occur during host execution because OpenCL compiles kernel source at runtime rather than at build time.

% src: quantitative_findings_qwen.json > canonical > direction_pass_rates > case_study + standard (omp_target pairs)
The OMP-target case studies suggest an abstraction gradient, though the small samples warrant caution. Stripping \texttt{\#pragma omp target} directives to recover plain OpenMP succeeds at 100\% ($n{=}9$), and converting OMP-target to explicit CUDA kernels passes at 66.7\% ($n{=}24$). In the reverse direction, generating correct target directives with data-mapping clauses from OpenMP reaches 44.4\% ($n{=}9$) but from CUDA reaches 0\% ($n{=}24$).
% src: quantitative_findings_qwen.json > canonical > direction_asymmetry: 5 McNemar tests, Bonferroni alpha=0.01, none significant
Five paired McNemar exact tests (Bonferroni-corrected $\alpha = 0.01$) do not reach statistical significance for direction asymmetry at the kernel level (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:stats-summary}). Given the small paired samples (3--24 paired kernels per test), these comparisons are inconclusive regarding whether the observed rate differences reflect systematic direction effects or sampling variability.

At the kernel level, difficulty is heterogeneous. Table~\ref{tab:per-kernel-main} shows the five easiest and five hardest kernels under \qwenshort{}. The five easiest kernels are all from HeCBench and feature regular stencil or graph patterns with structured loop nests; this reflects HeCBench's selection for kernel-level translation accessibility. The hard-fail set spans Rodinia and RSBench, featuring kernels whose difficulty we attribute to: cardiac tracking with irregular memory access patterns (\textit{heartwall}), stiff ODE integration with multi-file dependencies (\textit{myocyte}), B\textsuperscript{+}-tree traversal with pointer-heavy data structures (\textit{bptree}), cross-section lookup over multi-level nuclide grids (\textit{rsbench}), and particle-pair neighbor-list traversal (\textit{lavamd}). Five additional kernels have 0\% pass rates with fewer evaluation records ($n{=}6$ each). The full 31-kernel table is in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:per-kernel-full}.

% src: quantitative_findings_qwen.json > canonical > failure_taxonomy: BUILD_FAIL=245, RUN_FAIL=121, VERIFY_FAIL=29, EXTRACTION_FAIL=1, total_failures=396
Build-stage adaptation is the dominant failure mode. \buildfail{} accounts for 245 of 626 records (39.1\%) and 61.9\% of all 396 failures (Table~\ref{tab:overall-pass}, Figure~\ref{fig:failure-taxonomy}). These failures indicate incomplete API-surface adaptation: retained CUDA identifiers in OpenMP translations, missing OpenCL address-space qualifiers, or mismatched target-side dependencies.

\verifyfail{} is smaller but important: 29 of 626 records (4.6\%) compile and run but fail correctness. These cases demonstrate that compilation alone is insufficient: exit-code-only or compile-only checks would misclassify them as successful translations. \runfail{} accounts for 121 records (19.3\%), and a single \extractionfail{} (0.2\%) occurred where the LLM response could not be parsed into target files. Detailed failure case studies appear in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-c}.

If baseline success were driven primarily by surface-form memorization, augmented source variants should degrade pass rates. On a balanced 12-kernel CUDA-to-OpenMP subset present at all five augmentation levels, pass rates remain between 75\% and 83\% at L1--L4 (Table~\ref{tab:aug-balanced}). The L0 rate on this subset (80.6\%) exceeds the overall CUDA-to-OpenMP L0 rate (40.3\%) because the ablation filter selects kernels that already pass at L0, creating a survivorship bias toward easier kernels. L0 has higher counts because the canonical evaluation generates three samples per task (36 records for 12 kernels), while L1--L4 each generate one sample per kernel.

We computed Cochran-Armitage trend tests, but two confounds undermine their interpretability: the 3:1 ratio of L0 to L1--L4 sample sizes on this subset (8.5:1 in the full evaluation), and the survivorship selection inherent in L0-conditional ablation. We therefore report only descriptive rates for L1--L4. With $n{=}12$ per level and a baseline near 80\%, the analysis is powered to detect only a large drop (roughly to 45\%) at 80\% power; moderate effects would likely go undetected. The observed stability is compatible with some robustness to surface-form perturbation, though it does not rule out memorization at other levels of abstraction. The small, direction-restricted ($\text{CUDA}{\to}\text{OMP}$ only) sample limits generalizability. Testing on the highest-rate standard direction (CUDA-to-OpenMP) provides a conservative bound on degradation but a weak test of memorization-dependence for harder directions. Full per-kernel augmentation heatmaps appear in Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:aug-heatmap}.
