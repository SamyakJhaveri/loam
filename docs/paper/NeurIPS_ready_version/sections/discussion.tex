\section{Discussion, Limitations, and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\parbench{} establishes that LLMs can perform non-trivial parallel code translation when evaluation isolates the kernel and preserves executable correctness. Under stochastic sampling, \qwenshort{} achieves 36.7\% overall and 40.3\% on CUDA-to-OpenMP, with pass@1\,=\,23.9\% and pass@3\,=\,35.2\% across 142 unique L0 tasks. The dominant failure mode is incomplete API-surface adaptation at build time (39.1\% of all outcomes), not semantic reasoning errors. Direction-level results matter: aggregate pass rates conceal that CUDA-to-OpenMP is substantially more tractable than OpenCL-to-CUDA (0\%), and augmentation robustness analysis on a balanced 12-kernel subset shows stable pass rates (75--83\%) across five augmentation levels.

\textbf{Limitations.} The benchmark is correctness-focused: it does not measure speedups, occupancy, memory bandwidth, or efficiency. Correct translated code may still be unusably slow, a concern also raised by TRACY~\cite{TRACY2025}. The corpus is finite; some directions have small paired samples, and the augmentation analysis is limited to 12 kernels at L1--L4, precluding formal trend tests. Rodinia and related suites are likely present in training data, and augmentation tests surface-form robustness rather than deeper algorithmic memorization. The stochastic evaluation (temperature~0.7, three samples) captures variability but does not include self-repair; bounded self-repair is a framework capability deferred to future work. Results currently cover one complete model (\qwenshort{}) with \gptnew{} evaluation in progress.

\textbf{Future work.} Completing the \gptnew{} evaluation will enable cross-model comparison. Additional directions include self-repair experiments using the framework's built-in feedback loop, evaluation of agentic systems such as LASSI~\cite{LASSI2024}, fine-tuned HPC models~\cite{HPCCoderV2}, efficiency-aware evaluation following TRACY~\cite{TRACY2025}, and expansion of the augmentation campaign to more kernels and directions.

\parbench{} is evaluation infrastructure rather than a new translation method. Its contribution is to make the measurement problem sharper: kernel-centric isolation avoids repository-level build confounds, declarative specs make correctness reproducible, augmentation probes robustness, and the harness exposes where translations fail. The initial results show that current LLMs have real but uneven parallel translation capability, with build adaptation, direction asymmetry, and multi-file coordination as central barriers to reliable use.
